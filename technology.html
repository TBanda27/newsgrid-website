What is 5G today and what the future holds

If you ask the average person what they know about 5G technology, the first thing that will pop into their head will likely be something to do with cellphone technology. And if you consider the 2019 rollout of 5G thus far, perhaps that wouldn’t be too far off the mark. The average Jane or Joe would also likely recall that 5G is a faster follow-on technology to 4G LTE, and that wouldn’t be all that inaccurate either. However, what 5G offers today, how it’s deployed and what the 5G landscape is going to look like in the next 2–3 years, will be vastly different. In fact, at the risk of using clichés, indeed 5G NR (New Radio) technology is poised to be “disruptive.” That said, this next generation of wireless network technology will also be an enabler across a myriad of industries and applications, and in all likelihood spur all-new use cases as well.

Intel takes the lead.

Is Intel suddenly setting the standard for setting a majority standard for approving directors? The microchip giant made headlines Jan. 19 with the announcement that it had amended company by-laws to adopt a majority-vote requirement for directors in uncontested elections. When directors fail to win a majority, they must submit their resignations to the board or directors, which must accept or reject it within 90 days. Since Intel’s move, at least a half-dozen major business—including Dell Computer, Texas Instruments, United Technologies and PepsiCo—have embraced similar policies. The Intel policy is also emerging as an alternative to the so-called “modified plurality” model pioneered by Pfizer Corp., which had been gaining popularity since its introduction last year..
